As a REIT, it's legally required to distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders — which is why capacity expansion gets funded through debt and equity issuance rather than retained earnings. Understanding this financing structure explains why land/power acquisition timelines and capital raises are always in the background of Equinix's growth story.

## Competitors
- **CoreWeave locks in $40B in new commitments, backlog nears $100B, but stock dips on Meta cloud threat** — CoreWeave secured long-term power (200MW live, 526MW committed across three phases) via a Galaxy Digital lease for its Helios campus, and was added to the Nasdaq-100. Shares still fell nearly 14% after a Bloomberg report that Meta plans to sell excess AI cloud capacity — a signal that hyperscalers turning into compute resellers could squeeze "neocloud" pure-plays like CoreWeave. [source](https://finance.yahoo.com/markets/stocks/articles/coreweave-crwv-secures-helios-power-182144925.html)
> **In Practice · Neocloud vs. hyperscaler vs. colo.** A colo provider (Equinix) rents space/power/interconnection and lets tenants bring their own gear. A hyperscaler (AWS/Azure/GCP) owns everything end-to-end and sells managed cloud services. A "neocloud" (CoreWeave, Lambda) sits in between — owns GPU fleets, leases the building/power, and sells raw AI compute. Meta reselling excess capacity blurs the hyperscaler/neocloud line and is exactly the kind of competitive dynamic worth tracking as capacity math shifts.

The pause buys time for a regulatory framework requiring operators to pay into grid-investment funds — a template other states may copy. [source](https://www.axios.com/2026/07/14/ny-gov-kathy-hochul-data-center-moratorium-executive-order)
- **Virginia's new data center electricity tax takes effect** — As of July 1, Virginia charges $0.011/kWh on all data center electricity consumption, projected to raise ~$600M/year for the general fund. Virginia (Data Center Alley) choosing to tax rather than restrict is a notably different posture than New York's moratorium. [source](https://www.datacenterknowledge.com/data-center-construction/new-data-center-developments-july-2026)
> **In Practice · Why cooling dominates the power bill.** During the current U.S. heatwave, cooling alone is consuming up to 40% of a data center's electricity — and that share climbs as outside temps rise, since chillers work harder against a higher delta-T. This is the practical reason liquid/direct-to-chip cooling is displacing air-cooled CRAC units for high-density racks: moving heat via liquid is dramatically more efficient than moving it via air, which directly lowers PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness — total facility power ÷ IT equipment power).

## AI & Compute Demand
- **PJM asks DOE to force data centers onto backup generators during grid emergencies** — With a severe U.S. heatwave straining supply, PJM Interconnection (the largest U.S. grid operator) requested authority to order data centers to switch to on-site backup power within 15 minutes of an emergency signal, freeing grid capacity for homes and businesses. U.S. data centers already draw ~4% of national power (heading to ~9% by 2030), and a single hyperscale site can use as much water as a city of 50,000. [source](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/7/3/us-heatwave-raises-alarms-over-ai-data-centre-energy-demands)
- **Gartner: data center electricity demand up 26% in 2026, AI servers alone up 84%** — Global data center power demand is projected to hit 132GW in 2026 (up from 104GW), with AI-optimized servers jumping from 95 to 175 TWh of consumption. Gartner's framing lines up with the "power-bound, not GPU-bound" theme showing up across the industry — at least 75 U.S. data center projects worth $130B have been delayed or canceled for lack of power, not lack of chips or capital. [source](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2026-06-10-gartner-says-data-center-electricity-demand-to-grow-26-percent-in-2026)
> **In Practice · Power is the new capacity planning bottleneck.** For years, GPU supply was the constraint on AI buildouts; now it's grid interconnection queues and substation lead times (often 3-5+ years). When you see a hyperscaler announce a "10 GW" deal, that's the real ceiling on how much compute they can ever turn on at that site — translate MW commitments into rough GPU-cluster scale (a 100MW site supports roughly tens of thousands of high-end GPUs) to gauge how serious an announcement actually is.

[source](https://llm-stats.com/ai-news)
> **In Practice · Reading a model release like a spec sheet, not a press release.** Look past the headline for three numbers: context window (how much text/code it can hold in one pass), the benchmark suite used (SWE-bench for coding, MMLU for general knowledge — and whether it's the harder "verified" variant), and price per million tokens. A model that's "smarter" but costs 3x more per token isn't necessarily the better engineering choice for a given workload — that's the same cost/benefit tradeoff you'll make on a project business case.

## Networking
- **Cisco Live 2026 pushes "AI-native" networking and 800G→1.6T silicon roadmap** — Cisco drew a hard line between legacy "AI-assisted" features and networks built to be self-aware and self-healing from the ground up. Headline products: the Cisco 8223 (a 51.2 Tbps router for distributed AI workloads), the N9100 switch series pairing Cisco Silicon One with Nvidia Spectrum-4 ASICs for 800G Ethernet, and a next-gen Silicon One G-series architecture designed to scale from 800G to 1.6T ports for AI training clusters. [source](https://www.networkworld.com/article/4113462/ciscos-2026-agenda-prioritizes-ai-ready-infrastructure-connectivity.html)
> **In Practice · Why AI clusters need different fabric than a normal data center.** Traditional east-west traffic in an enterprise DC is bursty and forgiving of some latency/packet loss. GPU training traffic (all-to-all collective operations across thousands of GPUs) needs a lossless, low-latency, non-blocking spine-leaf (Clos) fabric — a single dropped packet stalls the whole training job. That's why RoCE (RDMA over Converged Ethernet) and ultra-high port-density switches like the N9100 exist: they're purpose-built for GPU-to-GPU traffic patterns, not general enterprise networking.

> **In Practice · Why leaked cloud keys are worse than leaked passwords.** An Azure PAT (Personal Access Token) or storage access key isn't just a login — it's a scoped credential that can be embedded in CI/CD pipelines, scripts, and config files, often with far broader permissions than a human account and no MFA prompt to stop misuse. This is the real argument for short-lived tokens and secrets managers (Azure Key Vault, HashiCorp Vault) over long-lived static keys checked into repos: it shrinks the blast radius when — not if — a repo gets exposed.

> **In Practice · Risk vs. Issue — say the right word.** A risk is an uncertain future event that may or may not happen (the NY moratorium becoming permanent, a vendor missing a milestone). An issue is something that has already happened and needs resolution now (a permit denial that already landed). Conflating the two in a status report is a fast way to lose stakeholder trust — risks belong in a risk register with probability/impact and a response plan; issues belong on an action log with an owner and a due date.
