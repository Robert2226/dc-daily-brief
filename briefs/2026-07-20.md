## Equinix
> **In Practice · Cross-connects are the actual product, not the building.** It's easy to think of a colo provider like Equinix as a real-estate company that happens to hold servers, but the durable moat is the interconnection layer: physical or virtual cross-connects between one customer's cage and another's (a bank to its market-data feed, an enterprise to an AWS Direct Connect port) inside the same facility. Power and space are commodities any developer can build; a dense, already-populated ecosystem of the *other* networks and clouds you need to reach isn't — that's why Equinix's "who else is already in this building" pitch matters more to sticky revenue than raw megawatts. When you're evaluating a facility (xScale or retail colo), the interconnection density and named ecosystem partners are the harder-to-replicate asset, not the shell.

## DC Infrastructure
- **FERC's grid-reform deadline lands today** — Generation adequacy reports are due July 20 from all six regional grid operators (PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, ISO-NE, NYISO), the first checkpoint under FERC's June 18 show-cause orders requiring each to justify or reform how data centers and other large loads interconnect — with tariff revisions due within 60 days of the original order. [FERC](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-launches-aggressive-targeted-action-speed-large-load-integration)
- **First coordinated nationwide protest wave hits data centers** — HumansFirst, co-founded by former Tea Party organizer Amy Kremer, coordinated 142 protests across 42 states on July 18 — Texas (18 events), Georgia (11), and California (8) led — citing water use, grid cost pass-through, and lack of community input; polling cited in coverage puts only 14% of Americans willing to support a data center in their own community. [Daily Signal](https://www.dailysignal.com/2026/07/19/data-center-protest-nationwide/)
> **In Practice · Section 206 is FERC's tool for forcing a rule change, not just reviewing one.** Most FERC action approves or rejects tariffs utilities propose. A Section 206 "show cause" order flips that: FERC asserts an *existing* rule may no longer be just and reasonable and puts the burden on the grid operator to either defend it or rewrite it on FERC's clock. Ordering all six RTOs/ISOs simultaneously, rather than acting case-by-case as complaints arise, signals FERC treats large-load interconnection as a systemic problem, not a one-off dispute — worth knowing the difference between "FERC reviewed a filing" and "FERC forced one" when you're reading grid-policy coverage.

## AI & Compute Demand
- **Combined hyperscaler capex nears $690B for 2026, almost double 2025** — Amazon ($200B), Alphabet ($175–185B), Microsoft ($120B+), Meta ($115–135B), and Oracle ($50B) are on pace for $660–690B combined this year. The demand behind it shows up as backlog: Oracle's remaining performance obligations hit $523B, Alphabet Cloud's backlog jumped 55% sequentially past $240B, and Microsoft disclosed an $80B Azure order backlog it says it *cannot* fulfill due to power constraints. [Futurum Group](https://futurumgroup.com/insights/ai-capex-2026-the-690b-infrastructure-sprint/)
> **In Practice · A backlog a company says it can't fulfill is a different signal than one it just hasn't billed yet.** Most backlog disclosures (Digital Realty's, CoreWeave's) describe signed contracts still working through construction — a timing problem. Microsoft explicitly attaching "power constraints" to $80B of unfulfillable Azure orders is a capacity-ceiling problem: no amount of faster construction execution fixes it if there's no interconnection available to energize the building. When you see a backlog number, the follow-up question is which kind it is — a construction queue that clears with time, or a hard external constraint that doesn't.

Google scrapping and rebuilding Gemini 3.5 Pro's *base model* over hallucination rates means the eval suite caught something the team judged unshippable — a governance decision, not a scheduling one. The tell is which lever a lab pulls: more compute buys you a later date on the same model; a rebuild means the thing that shipped internally didn't clear the bar at all.

## Networking
- **Nvidia pushes Spectrum-X into head-to-head competition with Cisco and Arista's core Ethernet business** — Nvidia's Spectrum-X Ethernet switch line is drawing direct demand from data center operators building AI clusters, positioning Nvidia — previously a GPU and InfiniBand vendor — as a full competitor in the Ethernet switch market both incumbents have long owned. [SDxCentral](https://www.sdxcentral.com/news/nvidia-storms-ethernet-switch-market-in-challenge-to-cisco-arista/)
- **HPE's Juniper QFX5250 ships as a fully liquid-cooled switch built for Nvidia Rubin and AMD MI400 racks** — Built on Broadcom Tomahawk 6 silicon with up to 102.4Tbps of Ethernet bandwidth, the QFX5250 is designed specifically to tie next-generation GPU racks together as air-cooled switching runs out of headroom at these port speeds. [Network World](https://www.networkworld.com/article/4099698/hpe-loads-up-ai-networking-portfolio-strengthens-nvidia-amd-partnerships.html)
> **In Practice · Scale-up vs. scale-out is the distinction that explains why Nvidia is suddenly a switch vendor.** "Scale-up" networking connects GPUs *within* a server or rack to act as one giant accelerator (NVLink/NVSwitch — Nvidia has always owned this layer). "Scale-out" networking connects racks and pods *to each other* across the data center floor — traditionally Ethernet or InfiniBand, and traditionally Cisco/Arista/Juniper territory. Spectrum-X is Nvidia extending from the layer it already controls into the layer it didn't, on the argument that AI traffic patterns (many-to-many, latency-sensitive) need the scale-out fabric co-designed with the GPU, not bought separately. That's the real stake in "Nvidia vs. Cisco" headlines — it's a bet about where the design boundary between compute and network should sit.

- **Broadcom upgrades to CNCF Platinum membership, joining AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, and Nvidia in the top tier** — Announced July 16, the move formalizes Broadcom's push around AI-ready cloud-native infrastructure, building on its decade-long position as a top-three Kubernetes contributor and its work on Velero, the Kubernetes-native backup/DR project used to protect AI workload state across clusters. - **Sophos: phishing and malicious email overtook unpatched vulnerabilities as the top ransomware root cause** — Sophos' State of Ransomware 2026 report (2,158 orgs surveyed across 17 countries) found malicious email (26%) and phishing (24%) now outrank vulnerability exploitation (18%, down from 32%) as ransomware entry points — and that MFA was present in 97% of credential-based attacks that succeeded anyway. [Dark Reading](https://www.darkreading.com/identity-access-management-security/identity-attacks-overtake-exploits-top-ransomware-cause)
> **In Practice · MFA present isn't the same as MFA that resists phishing.** Push-notification and SMS/OTP-based MFA still fundamentally trusts the user to make a correct judgment call under pressure — "approve this login" push-bombing and real-time phishing proxies (adversary-in-the-middle kits) both exploit exactly that trust gap, which is why Sophos found MFA deployed in 97% of successful credential attacks. Phishing-*resistant* MFA (FIDO2/WebAuthn hardware keys or passkeys) removes the human judgment call entirely by cryptographically binding the credential to the legitimate site's domain, so a look-alike login page simply can't complete the handshake. If your org counts "MFA enabled" as a solved risk without asking which *kind*, this report is the data point that assumption is wrong.

## Program & PM
- **PMI Agile 2026 sets its program: human-centricity as the counterweight to AI-driven complexity** — The July 26–28 conference's theme, "MORE, Together — Shaping the Future with Agility," centers on a keynote from Dr. Johan Roos arguing that human-centricity is the load-bearing value of the Manifesto for Enterprise Agility precisely *because* AI raises the stakes on judgment, curiosity, and collaboration rather than removing the need for them. [Agile Alliance](https://agilealliance.org/pmi-agile-2026/)
> **In Practice · "Human-centricity" as a design principle, not a soft-skills add-on.** It's tempting to read AI-and-agility conference themes as reassurance messaging aimed at anxious practitioners, but the sharper version of the argument is structural: as AI agents absorb more of the mechanical coordination work (status chasing, schedule updates — the same shift driving PMI's ECO reweighting toward Business Environment), the judgment calls that remain for humans get *harder*, not easier, because they're the ones AI explicitly couldn't resolve. Practically, that means a team's investment in facilitation, stakeholder judgment, and cross-functional communication skills should scale up as automation increases, not down — the easy coordination work disappearing doesn't shrink the human skill requirement, it concentrates it.
