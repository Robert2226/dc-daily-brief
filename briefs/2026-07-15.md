The build-out is funded by a backlog that's grown nearly 300% year-over-year to $99.4B, with per-megawatt build costs now running $15–25M. [DCD](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/coreweave-aims-to-add-5gw-more-data-center-capacity-by-2030-anticipates-capex-in-2026-to-double/)
> **In Practice · Backlog isn't revenue.** A "$99.4B backlog" is contracted future revenue, not cash in hand — it depends on customers actually drawing down capacity as data centers come online, and on CoreWeave successfully financing $30B+ of annual capex against it (mostly debt, since a neocloud has none of a REIT's dividend-funded balance sheet). When you see backlog and capex numbers side by side like this, the real question for any project/business case is the same: is the funding secured, or is it a projection built on the backlog materializing?

## DC Infrastructure
- **Microsoft's $3.3B Mount Pleasant, Wisconsin campus comes fully online** — The facility is Microsoft's first data center at the site and is now serving production workloads, part of a broader wave of mega-projects: Amazon is putting $10B into a new Montgomery County campus and Google $15B into a separate US build, alongside a new Google data center in Horndal, Sweden. [Data Center Knowledge](https://www.datacenterknowledge.com/data-center-construction/new-data-center-developments-july-2026)
- **Virginia regulators weigh forcing data centers to pay for their own transmission lines** — Two weeks after Virginia's new $0.011/kWh data center electricity tax took effect, the State Corporation Commission is now considering a Dominion Energy rate case proposal — backed by consumer advocates and Gov. Spanberger's administration — that would make large "high-load" customers like data centers bear the cost of the dedicated high-voltage lines built solely to serve them. [Virginia Mercury](https://virginiamercury.com/2026/07/15/scc-considers-making-data-centers-shoulder-more-of-the-cost-of-transmission-lines/)
> **In Practice · Direct-assignment vs. rolled-in cost allocation.** Utility rate cases split infrastructure costs one of two ways: "rolled-in," where a new line's cost is spread across all ratepayers, or "direct-assigned," where the customer that triggered the build pays for it alone. Virginia's proposal is a push toward direct assignment for data centers specifically — the regulatory equivalent of a project sponsor being told to fund their own scope-driven infrastructure instead of it coming out of a shared budget. It's the same cost-ownership logic you'd apply on a program with one stakeholder driving disproportionate spend.

## AI & Compute Demand
- **PJM data center demand tagged as primary driver of $23B in customer price hikes through 2028** — A PJM market report found data center load is a "primary reason" for $23B in higher electricity costs that will hit ratepayers across PJM's 14-state footprint through at least 2028. Part of the problem: some data centers can shift consumption to dodge peak-demand periods used to allocate costs, while residential customers have no comparable advocate at the rate-setting table. [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/07/14/data-centers-23-billion-electricity-bills/)
- **PJM misses its own power supply procurement goal amid AI-driven surge** — The largest U.S. grid operator, serving 13 states plus D.C., recorded a $16.4B procurement bill for power supply — a new record, up over 60% — with data centers responsible for roughly $6.3B of that. [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-14/largest-us-grid-misses-power-supply-goal-amid-rampant-ai-surge)
> **In Practice · How a capacity auction actually works.** PJM doesn't just react to demand — it runs a forward capacity auction where generators bid to guarantee power years ahead, and the clearing price sets what everyone gets paid (and what customers get billed). When demand forecasts jump — as they have with AI load — the auction clears at a much higher price for *all* capacity, not just the new increment. That's the mechanism behind headlines like "$16.4B record" and "$23B in price hikes": it's not a direct pass-through of data center power bills, it's a market-wide price shift caused by tighter projected reserve margins.

## New AI Models & Releases
- **Claude Sonnet 5 becomes the default model across Claude.ai** — Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 5 launched June 30 and immediately replaced the prior default for Free and Pro users; Fable 5 (a specialized variant with tighter safety classifiers and stricter export-use conditions) returned across the Claude platform July 1. [llm-stats](https://llm-stats.com/llm-updates)
- **Open-weight models cluster around the same week: GLM-5.2, DeepSeek V4, Qwen 3.6** — All three shipped in the same window as the GPT-5.6/Grok 4.5/Sonnet 5 frontier releases, continuing the pattern of open-weight labs releasing in near-lockstep with closed frontier labs rather than trailing by months. [AI Release Tracker](https://aireleasetracker.com/latest)
> **In Practice · Frontier vs. open-weight — the real tradeoff.** A frontier API model (Claude, GPT, Grok) is billed per token with no infrastructure to manage — you trade cost-per-call for zero ops burden. An open-weight model (GLM, DeepSeek, Qwen) can be self-hosted, which means fixed infrastructure cost (GPUs, power, cooling — all things you already understand from the DC side) instead of variable per-token cost, plus full control over data residency. The breakeven point is usage volume: low/unpredictable volume favors API billing, sustained high volume favors owning the hardware — the same build-vs-buy math that shows up in any capital equipment decision.

## Networking
- **Cisco/Foundry: AI will triple enterprise network traffic within three years, most networks aren't ready** — A new joint report finds AI agents generate 450% more network traffic than human users, 73% of organizations expect to hit campus/branch capacity limits within 24 months, and only 15% currently have networks flexible enough to support AI at scale — a gap Cisco frames as now rivaling compute as the bottleneck to enterprise AI success. [Dataconomy](https://dataconomy.com/2026/07/15/cisco-ai-surge-to-triple-enterprise-network-traffic-by-2026/)
> **In Practice · Agent traffic doesn't look like human traffic.** A human clicking through an app generates bursty, session-based traffic with long idle gaps. An AI agent chains multiple API/tool calls per task, often in tight loops with retries — steady, high-frequency, machine-paced load that legacy campus networks (sized around human usage patterns) weren't built for. This is the campus/branch version of the same lesson the AI training fabric world already learned: when the traffic pattern changes, capacity planning has to be redone from the pattern up, not just scaled from historical averages.

## Backend / Cloud & Data
- **Kubernetes Dynamic Resource Allocation (DRA) reaches general availability in v1.35** — DRA lets pods request specialized hardware — GPUs, and other accelerators — with the same structured, driver-based model Kubernetes already uses for storage, replacing the older device-plugin approach that couldn't express fine-grained sharing or topology constraints. [Kubernetes Blog](https://kubernetes.io/blog/)
- **Microsoft ships record 570-flaw Patch Tuesday, including two exploited zero-days** — July's Patch Tuesday is Microsoft's largest on record by flaw count, with two vulnerabilities already under active exploitation and one more publicly disclosed before a patch existed. [SharkStriker](https://sharkstriker.com/blog/july-2026-data-breaches/)
> **In Practice · Why DRA matters if you've touched GPU scheduling.** Before DRA, getting a pod the right GPU meant vendor-specific device plugins with no way to express "give me 2 GPUs on the same NVLink island" or partition a GPU between workloads. DRA makes hardware requests structured and queryable the way persistent volume claims already are, so a scheduler can reason about topology and sharing instead of treating accelerators as opaque, indivisible resources. It's the same underlying idea as PDU/rack power capacity planning — you don't just count units, you have to know how they're physically wired together.

## Program & PM
- **PMI launches the overhauled PMP exam and PMBOK 8th edition, effective July 9** — This is the most significant PMP revision since the 2021 agile/hybrid overhaul. Domain weighting shifts to People 33% / Process 41% / Business Environment 26% (up sharply from 8%), new content areas cover AI, sustainability, and value delivery, and agile/hybrid approaches get more emphasis relative to predictive methods. PMI also widened the experience-eligibility window from 8 years to 10 years of qualifying project work. [PMI](https://www.pmi.org/certifications/project-management-pmp/new-exam)
> **In Practice · "Value delivery" over scope/schedule/cost — what that actually changes.** The old PMP mental model measured project success primarily against the iron triangle: did it hit scope, schedule, and cost targets? The July 2026 ECO reframes success around whether the project delivered the business value it was chartered to create — which can mean a project that ran over budget but unlocked the intended outcome is judged a success, and a project that hit every target but the outcome never mattered is not. For exam prep, that means practicing questions from "what benefit does this action realize" rather than "which constraint does this protect" — a real shift in how PMI wants candidates to reason, not just new vocabulary.
