TradingKey
In Practice · A price target isn't a prediction, it's a valuation model's output

When an analyst raises a target from $1,109 to $1,130, they aren't guessing where the stock goes — they're updating one input (usually forward EBITDA or revenue estimates) in a discounted cash flow or multiples model and reporting the new output. The number is only as good as the assumption behind it, which is why a stock can miss its "target" for quarters if the underlying growth assumption doesn't materialize. Read target changes as a proxy for "what did the analyst's model just learn," not as a forecast.

Kavout
In Practice · Backlog is a leading indicator — treat it like one, not like revenue

A colo or REIT's "backlog" is signed contracts for capacity that isn't delivering revenue yet, usually because the physical build (power, shell, cooling) isn't finished. A 95.8% YoY jump tells you demand is real and contracted, but it also tells you the company now has a construction-execution risk it didn't have before — the number converts to revenue only as fast as it can pour concrete and energize substations. When evaluating a growth story, always ask whether the headline number is booked, billing, or delivered.

In Practice · Community opposition is now a top-tier site-selection risk, not a permitting footnote

For years, data center site selection weighted power availability, fiber access, land cost, and tax incentives most heavily; local political risk was assumed manageable through community-benefit agreements. A voter-enacted ban with 86% support signals that assumption is breaking down in some markets — opposition isn't just NIMBYism about noise or traffic anymore, it's organized around grid cost pass-through (like Virginia's new tax) and water use. Any site-selection framework in 2026 needs a real political-risk score, weighted as heavily as power and fiber.

In Practice · "Unlimited demand" claims deserve the same scrutiny as any other capacity forecast

Executives have a structural incentive to describe demand as unlimited — it justifies capex and reassures investors financing the buildout. The useful move isn't to dismiss the claim, it's to ask what would falsify it: contracted backlog converting to signed, billing revenue (see today's Digital Realty item); utilization rates on already-built capacity; and whether power/permitting constraints (this week's Virginia and Monterey Park items) start capping growth regardless of demand. Treat "demand is unlimited" as a hypothesis to test against harder numbers, not a given.

Cisco Newsroom
In Practice · eBPF is why "patch without downtime" is now technically credible

Traditional patching replaces vulnerable code, which usually means restarting the affected process or rebooting. eBPF lets you attach small, sandboxed programs directly into the Linux kernel that observe or intercept specific system calls and network events without modifying or restarting anything — so a vendor can insert a narrow rule that blocks the exact exploit path (a bad syscall pattern, a malicious packet shape) while the real fix ships on a normal schedule. It's the same kernel-level hooking technology powering modern observability tools (Cilium, Falco) — one mechanism, security and networking use cases both.

CNCF
In Practice · What a CNI actually does, and why running two is a liability

A Container Network Interface plugin is what assigns pod IP addresses, programs routing rules, and enforces network policy inside a Kubernetes cluster — it's the layer between "my app has a service definition" and "packets actually move." Running two CNIs (common after a merger, multi-team cluster, or unresolved past migration) usually means duplicated policy engines, inconsistent observability, and higher troubleshooting overhead, since a dropped packet could originate in either one. Michelin's zero-downtime consolidation is the payoff of good migration sequencing — moving workloads incrementally behind existing policy rather than a hard cutover.
