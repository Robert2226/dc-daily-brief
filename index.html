01Equinix

Leadership reshuffle: CBO Jon Lin departs July 18, new CPO coming

Equinix confirmed Chief Business Officer Jon Lin will exit July 18 under the company's Executive Severance Plan; rather than naming an immediate replacement, Equinix is redistributing his responsibilities across the existing senior leadership team while it finalizes a new Chief Product Officer.

StreetInsider
In Practice · Interim continuity beats a rushed hire

When a senior leader exits, the instinct is to backfill the seat immediately — but spreading their responsibilities across incumbents for a defined interim period (rather than promoting under time pressure) preserves decision quality while the org actually vets a successor. The tell that a company is doing this well: specific named owners for each transferred responsibility, not a vague "the team will absorb it." If you're ever handed a project mid-transition, your first question should be who owns what today, not who the eventual replacement will be.

CoreWeave's biggest risk isn't demand — it's who's asking

CoreWeave shares are down roughly 23% since June 18 after a Bloomberg report that Meta plans to build out its own cloud infrastructure, threatening a customer relationship CoreWeave's backlog leans on heavily. Cathie Wood's ARK funds bought the dip anyway — $811,600 on July 8, on top of $2M (July 7) and $6.5M (June 29) in prior purchases.

Motley Fool
In Practice · Customer concentration is a risk-register line item, not a footnote

A ~$100B contracted backlog looks like security until you realize a handful of hyperscaler customers can become competitors overnight — Meta doesn't need to churn its CoreWeave contract to hurt CoreWeave, it just needs to stop being a future customer. When you're assessing vendor or partner stability on a project, ask what share of their revenue sits with your closest peers — concentrated revenue and concentrated risk are the same number.

03DC Infrastructure

Boulder City, NV appeals BLM's approval of a data center on federal land

The city council voted unanimously to appeal the Bureau of Land Management's June 26 approval of the Townsite Data Center on 81 acres of BLM land in Eldorado Valley, arguing BLM swept aside required local consultation and public-input process.

KTNV

Samsung Heavy Industries targets 2028 for floating AI data centers

Samsung is developing a standardized 50MW offshore data center platform — server halls, power, cooling, and onboard LNG generation on a single floating structure — aimed at sites where land, power, or permitting are the constraint. MOUs are in place with Capital (Greek shipbuilder), Lloyd's Register, and Dallas-based developer Mousterian.

TechRadar
In Practice · Federal land review doesn't erase local review — it stacks on top of it

BLM approving a project on federal land under NEPA satisfies federal environmental and use requirements, but it doesn't automatically override a municipality's own consultation rights when the project still needs local utilities, roads, and emergency services. Boulder City's appeal is a reminder that "who owns the land" and "who has to sign off" are different questions — a smart site-selection or approvals timeline maps every jurisdiction with standing, not just the one issuing the primary permit.

04AI & Compute Demand

US data center power demand set to double by 2027

Goldman Sachs projects US data center electricity demand climbing from 31 GW in 2025 to 41 GW in 2026 and 66 GW in 2027, driven almost entirely by AI training and inference buildout rather than general enterprise IT growth.

Goldman Sachs

Rack density is outrunning facility design

Current-generation AI sites now demand 100–750 MW per campus, with a single NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 rack drawing 120–140 kW — roughly 10x a traditional enterprise rack — forcing power distribution and liquid cooling retrofits mid-build rather than at initial design.

TechPlusTrends
In Practice · Forecasting exponential demand with linear infrastructure

Power grids, transformers, and permitting timelines scale roughly linearly — you can only build so much substation capacity per year. AI compute demand is scaling exponentially. Any plan that assumes next year looks like a straight-line extension of this year will underbuild; the Goldman Sachs 31→41→66 GW curve isn't a trend line, it's closer to a doubling period, and doubling periods need capacity ordered against the future curve, not the current one.

05New AI Models & Releases

Moonshot AI ships Kimi K3, the largest open-weight model yet

China's Moonshot AI released Kimi K3, a 2.8-trillion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model with a 1M-token context window, native visual understanding, and two new architecture components (Kimi Delta Attention, Attention Residuals) built for long-horizon coding and agent workloads. It still trails Claude and GPT-5.6 on raw benchmarks but undercuts them sharply on price ($0.30/$3/$15 per million cached/input/output tokens); full weights land July 27.

VentureBeat
In Practice · A "2.8 trillion parameter" model doesn't run 2.8 trillion parameters per request

Kimi K3 is a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) design: the full parameter count is split into many specialized "expert" sub-networks, and a router activates only a handful of them per token — so inference cost tracks the much smaller active parameter count, not the total. That's why a model this size can be priced competitively at all. When you see a huge parameter count in a model announcement, the number that actually predicts cost and latency is active parameters, not total parameters.

06Networking

Cisco Cloud Control goes global, anchoring the AgenticOps push

Cisco's unified platform for operating and defending IT infrastructure — built to let human teams and AI agents share one login and one control plane — moves from US-only controlled availability to global availability this month, forming the operational backbone of Cisco's new AgenticOps model.

ITP.net

Cisco moves to a twice-monthly, risk-based patch schedule — first advisories land ISE and RoomOS fixes

Cisco's July 15 disclosures covered Identity Services Engine and RoomOS vulnerabilities under its new predictable release cadence, alongside separately-disclosed ClamAV flaws that can crash Secure Endpoint's Windows connector.

Cisco PSIRT
In Practice · Supervised autonomy is the actual design goal, not full autonomy

AgenticOps-style platforms aren't trying to remove humans from infrastructure operations — they're trying to let an AI agent execute routine, well-defined actions (restart a service, roll back a config) while a human retains approval authority over anything higher-risk. The engineering problem is drawing that line correctly: too conservative and you've built an expensive dashboard, too permissive and an agent's bad decision propagates at machine speed. Any agentic-ops rollout plan should specify, action by action, which side of that line each task falls on before go-live.

07Backend / Cloud & Data

Critical Zoom flaw (CVSS 9.8) allows unauthenticated account takeover

CVE-2026-53412 affects Zoom Workplace and VDI clients for Windows: no credentials, phishing, or physical access required — just network reachability to an unpatched client. Zoom shipped fixes in Workplace 7.0.0 and VDI Client 7.0.10/6.6.15/6.5.18; no active exploitation reported as of publication.

The Hacker News

Dynamic Resource Allocation reaches GA in Kubernetes 1.35

DRA graduates to general availability, giving clusters a standardized way to request, share, and reclaim specialized hardware like GPUs — replacing ad hoc device-plugin hacks that many AI/ML platform teams had been running in production.

Fairwinds
In Practice · Reading a CVSS score like an ops engineer, not a checklist

A 9.8 isn't just "very bad" — it's the composite of attack vector (network-reachable), complexity (low), privileges required (none), and impact (high across confidentiality/integrity/availability). The Zoom flaw scores near-maximum precisely because every one of those sub-scores is worst-case: no foothold needed, no user interaction, full compromise. When triaging patches under time pressure, decompose the score instead of just comparing numbers — a 7.5 that needs local access patches on a normal cycle; a 9.8 that's network-reachable with no auth patches today.

08Program & PM

APM: build resilience into the plan, not just the response

In a July 7 piece for the Association for Project Management, Isaac John Bamfield argues for applying systems thinking — treating a project as an interconnected set of feedback loops rather than a linear task chain — to build resilience in ahead of disruption instead of reacting to it after the fact.

APM
In Practice · Feedback loops and leverage points, in plain terms

Systems thinking asks two questions a linear Gantt chart doesn't: where does an output loop back and become an input (a delay in permitting delays procurement, which delays a schedule review, which delays the next permitting decision), and where's the leverage point where a small change produces an outsized effect (fixing the approval bottleneck matters more than optimizing twenty downstream tasks that are all waiting on it). Before adding tasks to a recovery plan, trace one full loop through your project and find where it's actually stuck — that's usually a higher-leverage fix than adding parallel workstreams.
