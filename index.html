01Equinix

Equinix pays $41.5M to settle accounting-malpractice lawsuit

Equinix settled a shareholder class action alleging it misclassified routine maintenance expenses as capital expenditures to inflate Adjusted Funds From Operations (AFFO) — a manipulation the suit says padded executive bonuses by roughly $150M over five years. It closes out the last piece of legal overhang from a federal SEC/DOJ investigation that ended without charges in November 2025.

Data Center Dynamics
In Practice · What AFFO is, and why capex-vs-opex classification is where REIT numbers get gamed

Adjusted Funds From Operations is the metric REITs use instead of net income, because standard GAAP earnings get distorted by real-estate depreciation that doesn't reflect actual cash performance — AFFO adds depreciation back and subtracts recurring capital spending needed to maintain the properties. That subtraction is exactly the lever this suit alleges Equinix pulled backwards: classify a maintenance expense (which should reduce AFFO) as a capital expenditure (which doesn't) and the "cash available to shareholders" number goes up on paper without any real change underneath it. When a REIT's AFFO growth outpaces its reported maintenance capex growth for several quarters running, that gap is exactly what a forensic read of the filings should probe.

In Practice · A "junk bond" rating isn't a moral judgment — it's a specific claim about repayment risk

High-yield ("junk") simply means a credit rating agency has classified the bond below investment grade (BBB-/Baa3), because the issuer's cash flows are seen as less certain or more leveraged relative to the debt — investors demand a higher interest rate to compensate. That Galaxy Digital's Helios financing needs to go the high-yield route, while BlackRock's Meta-backed El Paso bond (covered July 21) can likely price at investment-grade, tells you something about the underlying tenant: Meta's lease payments are viewed as near-certain, while CoreWeave's are viewed as riskier despite its scale. Same asset class, same "data center bond" headline — very different credit story underneath.

03DC Infrastructure

Oracle sues Wisconsin regulators over $7B collateral demand for its data center power plant

Oracle filed suit June 19 against the Wisconsin Public Service Commission, challenging a rule that requires large-load customers below an A-/A3 credit rating (Oracle sits at BBB) to post cash or letters of credit securing their power obligations — a requirement Oracle says could force it to post over $7B in collateral and cost $100M+ annually, tied to its Lighthouse campus in Port Washington.

Data Center Knowledge
In Practice · Why a utility cares about a tenant's bond rating before it builds a power plant

When a utility agrees to build bespoke generation for one customer's data center, it's taking on construction debt that only pays off if that customer keeps paying for decades — so regulators increasingly tie the deal's financial security requirement to the customer's own creditworthiness, not just a signed contract. A- and above is treated as reliable enough to skip collateral; below that, the utility (and ultimately other ratepayers) is exposed if the customer's business changes. Oracle's BBB rating is investment-grade and perfectly normal for a company its size — the dispute isn't about Oracle's solvency, it's about who bears the tail risk if an AI-demand assumption a decade out doesn't hold.

04AI & Compute Demand

Morgan Stanley: US data centers face a 44–49GW power shortfall through 2028 — up to 20% of projected demand

Distinct from Goldman's demand-growth curve (31→41→66GW), this is a supply-gap estimate: even accounting for planned grid buildout, Morgan Stanley sees a fifth of AI data center power demand going unmet by 2028 absent "time-to-power" workarounds that skip the standard multi-year interconnection queue.

Morgan Stanley via Investing.com
In Practice · "Demand" and "shortfall" are two different forecasts, and conflating them hides the actual constraint

A demand forecast (Goldman's 66GW by 2027) answers "how much power will data centers want to use." A shortfall forecast answers a harder question: of that demand, how much won't get built in time, given real-world limits on transformers, transmission lines, and permitting. Morgan Stanley's explicit hedge — that "time-to-power" solutions like the substation-sited micro data centers covered here July 21 could shrink the gap — is the tell that the shortfall number is a base case, not a hard ceiling. When you see competing power forecasts from different banks, check whether they're modeling appetite or delivery; they answer different questions and aren't in conflict just because the numbers differ.

05New AI Models & Releases

Google ships three new Gemini models — still no 3.5 Pro

Google released Gemini 3.6 Flash, 3.5 Flash-Lite, and Gemini 3.5 Flash Cyber (a security-tuned variant for vulnerability discovery and patching, limited to governments and trusted partners under the CodeMender program) on July 21. The flagship 3.5 Pro remains absent for a third straight missed date (last covered here July 20), while Google confirmed it has begun pretraining Gemini 4.

TechCrunch
In Practice · Why a lab ships a "Flash" tier at all instead of just one best model

Every major lab now maintains a family of models at different size/speed/cost points off the same base architecture — Flash/Flash-Lite versus Pro/Ultra — because most production use cases (classification, routing, high-volume agent calls) are latency- and cost-bound, not capability-bound; paying flagship-model prices for a task a smaller model handles fine is pure waste. Shipping the Flash tier while Pro is still gated behind quality review also lets Google keep revenue and developer mindshare moving without shipping a flagship that hasn't cleared its own bar. When you evaluate "which AI model to use" for a task, the first question is almost never "which is smartest" — it's which tier's ceiling is high enough for the job at the lowest cost.

BigGo Finance
In Practice · Supplier concentration is customer concentration's mirror image, and just as easy to miss

Earlier this week we covered how CoreWeave's revenue concentration in a few hyperscaler customers is a risk-register line item (July 17, 19). The same logic runs the other way: if one company holds "over 50% of Nvidia's optical module wallet," Nvidia has a single point of failure on a component every AI cluster needs to move data between GPUs — a factory fire, export-control action, or quality failure at Zhongji doesn't just hurt Zhongji, it can stall AI cluster buildouts industry-wide. When you're mapping risk in any supply chain, draw the dependency graph in both directions — who depends on you, and who you depend on — because a concentration risk assessment that only looks at customers is only half the picture.

07Backend / Cloud & Data

Critical SharePoint RCE (CVSS 9.8) under active exploitation after public PoC leaks

CVE-2026-50522 lets an unauthenticated attacker execute code on on-premises SharePoint servers via a malformed WS-Federation sign-in request; attackers are using it to steal SharePoint machine keys for long-term persistence. Microsoft patched it in July's update, but researchers stress that patching alone doesn't undo a prior compromise — exposed servers need machine-key rotation.

The Hacker News

CNCF ships Dragonfly v2.5.0 with direct Hugging Face and ModelScope downloads

The peer-to-peer file distribution project used to pull large container images and model weights efficiently across clusters added native support for pulling directly from the two dominant AI model registries, cutting a manual export/re-upload step out of ML platform pipelines.

CNCF
In Practice · Why "patch the CVE" and "the incident is over" are two different milestones

A patch closes the door the attacker used to get in — it does nothing about anything they took or planted before the door closed. SharePoint's machine keys are used to sign and validate ViewState and authentication tokens; an attacker who stole them before patching can keep forging valid session tokens indefinitely, walking straight past a "fixed" system with credentials that still work. That's why credible advisories for this class of bug always pair "apply the patch" with "rotate the secrets" — treating the vulnerability and the potential compromise as two separate problems requiring two separate fixes, on two different urgency clocks.

08Program & PM

BCG consultants Julia Dhar, Kristy Ellmer, and Philip Jameson argue most transformation efforts fail not from poor execution but because senior leaders believe they agree on why, what, and how to change — without ever testing that belief — drawing on a Pandora case study; a Forbes rebuttal from Steve Denning argues the framework understates how agile/adaptive methods already solve this. Forbes rebuttal
In Practice · Surfacing false alignment is a facilitation technique, not a personality trait some leaders have and others don't

The trap forms because disagreeing in a room full of senior peers carries social cost, and "sounds right, let's move" is the path of least resistance under time pressure — so alignment gets assumed rather than tested. The practical fix scales down to any project kickoff: instead of asking "are we aligned?" (which invites a reflexive yes), have each stakeholder independently answer the why/what/how questions in writing before the group discussion, then compare answers out loud. Divergence that would have stayed hidden in a verbal go-around becomes visible and addressable before it turns into a mid-project scope fight — the earlier the gap surfaces, the cheaper it is to close.
