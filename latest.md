> **In Practice · Colo vs. hyperscale vs. neocloud.** *Colo* (Equinix, Digital Realty) leases space/power and sells interconnection; *hyperscalers* (AWS/Azure/Google) build their own halls to run their own cloud; *neoclouds* (CoreWeave) are new GPU-only clouds that often *rent* capacity from others and resell it as AI compute. Knowing which model a player uses tells you how they compete — on network density, on scale, or on GPU availability.

## DC Infrastructure
- **NVIDIA unveils liquid cooling that runs "hotter than a hot tub," cutting water use up to 100%** — Warmer coolant lets data centers shed water-hungry chillers, a direct answer to AI's mounting water/PUE scrutiny. Sustainability caveats remain, but it's where direct-to-chip is heading — squarely your liquid-to-chip wheelhouse. [Tom's Hardware](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/data-centers/nvidia-announces-liquid-cooling-system-that-runs-hotter-than-a-hot-tub-promises-to-reduce-electricity-consumption-and-cut-water-use-by-up-to-100-percent-but-sustainability-challenges-remain)
- **Liquid cooling on track to double air-cooled capacity by end of 2026** — Direct-to-chip is now default for high-density builds (Rittal showed a 1MW unit supporting ~250kW/rack), and skidded modular 2MW cooling units are becoming standard kit. Air cooling is now the exception for AI halls. [TechRepublic](https://www.techrepublic.com/article/news-ai-data-center-power-cooling-infrastructure-dcw/)
> **In Practice · PUE, quickly.** Power Usage Effectiveness = total facility power ÷ IT power. A PUE of 1.5 means 50% overhead (cooling, losses) on top of the compute; 1.1 is elite. Liquid cooling wins partly because it slashes the cooling share of that overhead — so when a vendor touts water/energy savings, translate it to "lower PUE" to compare apples to apples.

## AI & Compute Demand
- **Power, not chips, is the binding constraint — US data center demand jumped from 23GW (2023) to 42GW (2026)** — The DOE projects data centers could hit ~12% of US electricity by 2028, and AI racks now pull 50–100kW vs 5–10kW for legacy. Natural gas is emerging as the near-term bridge while grid interconnects lag. Your critical-power depth is exactly the scarce skill this shortage rewards. [TechRepublic](https://www.techrepublic.com/article/news-ai-data-center-power-cooling-infrastructure-dcw/)
> **In Practice · Reading demand in megawatts.** The industry now measures AI capacity in MW of IT load, not square feet. Rough intuition: 1MW powers on the order of ~500–1,000 modern GPUs once you include cooling and overhead — so a "250MW lease" is a genuinely huge compute cluster. When you see deal sizes in MW, mentally convert to "how many GPUs and how much grid" to gauge scale.

It's a better signal than generic "IQ" benchmarks for coding usefulness, so when labs quote it, they're claiming practical software-engineering ability.

## Networking
- **Cisco unveils 102.4Tbps N9100 (NVIDIA Spectrum-6) and 800G switches for AI fabrics** — Cisco's new switches, co-engineered with NVIDIA, target ultra-low-latency AI training backends; 400G is now "entry-level" and 800G a mandatory requirement. CEO Chuck Robbins said AI will *triple* networking traffic within three years — a supercycle for data center networking. [Network World](https://www.networkworld.com/article/4080722/cisco-nvidia-strengthen-ai-ties-with-new-data-center-switch-reference-architectures.html)
> **In Practice · Spine-leaf (Clos) fabric.** AI clusters ditched the old three-tier network for a flat *spine-leaf* design: every "leaf" (rack) switch connects to every "spine" switch, so any server is a predictable two hops from any other — critical when thousands of GPUs must exchange gradients in lockstep. It's why AI backends need lossless, high-radix 800G Ethernet: one slow or congested link stalls the whole training job. [Cisco Blogs](https://blogs.cisco.com/networking)

## Backend / Cloud & Data
- **Kubernetes plateaus at ~68% of production workloads as teams split to serverless** — Adoption dipped from its 2024 peak as workloads move to serverless container platforms (AWS Fargate, Google Cloud Run, Azure Container Apps); Amazon EKS now lets you *reverse* a cluster upgrade within 7 days. Meanwhile Red Hat found 45% of Kubernetes incidents stem from misconfiguration. [CNCF](https://www.cncf.io/blog/2026/07/03/how-data-sovereignty-is-changing-cloud-native-infrastructure-design/)
> **In Practice · The shared responsibility model.** Gartner projects 99% of cloud security failures through 2026 are the *customer's* fault — almost always misconfiguration. The cloud provider secures the infrastructure ("security *of* the cloud"); you secure what you put in it — IAM, network rules, data ("security *in* the cloud"). Most breaches aren't clever hacks; they're an exposed storage bucket or an over-permissive role.

> **In Practice · Risk vs. Issue.** A *risk* might happen (future, uncertain — manage with a mitigation/response plan); an *issue* is happening now (certain — manage with a corrective action and an owner). Sloppy PMs call everything a "risk," hiding problems that need action today. In status updates, split them: "Top risks (watching)" vs. "Open issues (acting)" — it signals control and maps straight to the PMP's Process and People domains.
